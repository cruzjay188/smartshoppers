Decisions
The Decision

Calling it "Health Mode" implied moral judgment — categorizing items as healthy or unhealthy is subjective and culturally biased.

The Reasoning

The feature needed to feel supportive, not preachy. A name that moralizes gets rejected — users associate it with diet culture.

The Outcome

Snack Mode shifts focus from judgment → intention. It doesn't tell you what's healthy — it holds you to the limit you set.

The Decision

One alert is too easy to dismiss. Fully blocking the user is paternalistic. We needed teeth — not a wall.

The Reasoning

Two-strike flow: first alert skips while limit stays active. Second trigger forces a binary — turn off Snack Mode, or commit.

The Outcome

The interaction is intentional and hard to ignore — without being controlling. Mirrors best-in-class habit apps.

The Decision

A simpler device forced us to only build what mattered. Every button press had to justify its existence.

The Reasoning

Constraints were a UX tool, not a liability. Three buttons, three modes. No room for scope creep.

The Outcome

One mental model. The hardware limit became the product's clarity — a proof-of-concept without expensive infrastructure.

The Decision

Rolling Micro:bit text was too slow to confirm active modes. We needed to test the concept before investing in hardware.

The Reasoning

Hand-cut paper icons — green = active, black = inactive — swapped by a team member during live testing sessions.

The Outcome

Users immediately understood cart state from the icons. Validated the indicator pattern for production — zero display code written.

The Decision

Requiring a member ID at checkout excluded non-subscribers and added friction at the most critical moment.

The Reasoning

Any barrier at checkout kills adoption. The system must work for anyone who grabs a cart — subscriber or not.

The Outcome

Inclusive by default. Non-subscribers sync later via time-sensitive receipt code. Guest mode — zero account creation.

The Decision

A feature that helps shoppers spend less directly contradicts what most retailers want. No reframe = no business case.

The Reasoning

Shoppers who feel helped — not sold to — return more often and become higher-LTV customers over time.

The Outcome

SmartShopper doesn't reduce spending — it builds trust. That reframe is what makes this pilotable.
